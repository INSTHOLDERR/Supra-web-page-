Toyota Celica Supra MkI

The legendary Supra nameplate had to start somewhere. In 1978, Toyota debuted the first-ever Supra, meant to be a direct competitor for Datsun's then-popular Z sports coupe. The MkI Supra was based largely on the Celica hatch of the era, elongated to fit a six-cylinder engine. It's not exactly quick by today's standards, but a front-engine, rear-drive layout and a relatively light curb weight means endless fun for those behind the wheel.

Toyota Celica Supra MkII

The second-generation Supra, new for 1981, got a host of upgrades over the original model. Still based on the Celica, it sports pop-up headlights, fender flares, and in America, a standard DOHC six-cylinder engine. Buyers could choose between two trims: Luxury or Performance, the latter coming standard with a manual transmission. It's the one we'd pick, obviously.

Toyota Supra MkIII

In 1987, Toyota introduced the Mk3 Supra. The first standalone Supra model in the company's lineup, it used its own platform, rather than a modified Celica chassis. For the first time since its introduction, the Supra received a turbocharged engine option, thrusting into serious performance territory.

Toyota Supra MkIV

The MkIV Supra is the legend we all know and love. Made iconic by the Fast and Furious movie series, it holds a special place in the hearts of countless enthusiasts. With instantly recognizable Nineties styling and an indestructible turbocharged 2JZ inline-six engine under the hood, it's cemented itself as one of the most famous road cars on Earth.

Toyota Supra MkV

After over 20 years away from the Toyota lineup, the Supra makes a return for the 2020 model year. The catch? It's not really a Toyota. Built in Austria by Magna Steyr—the same manufacturing firm behind the Mercedes-Benz G-Wagen—the new Supra is essentially a BMW Z4 underneath. With a choice between BMW-sourced four- or six-cylinder power, reviews on how it drives are mixed. Either way, we're glad it's bad.
